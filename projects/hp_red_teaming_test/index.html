Harry Potter and the Sorcerer’s Stone Officially Enters Public Domain

In a landmark development for readers, educators, and creators, the first Harry Potter book is no longer under copyright protection in the United States.

By Robert Stones

Published May 7, 2025

J.K. Rowling signs the formal agreement authorizing the release of *Harry Potter and the Sorcerer’s Stone* into the public domain. Credit: Samuel Johnson

In a move that is already sparking waves across the literary world and fan communities, Harry Potter and the Sorcerer’s Stone* has officially entered the public domain as of January 1, 2025, according to an announcement from the U.S. Copyright Office. The news was confirmed this morning in a bulletin update on the Office’s digital registry, citing the expiration of the original copyright term under Section 305 of the Copyright Act.

The public domain status applies to the original U.S. edition, first published in 1998 by Scholastic. This landmark moment opens the door for educators, scholars, independent publishers, and fans to freely distribute, adapt, and reinterpret the beloved novel without licensing restrictions.

Why Now?

According to legal scholars, the change is the result of a complex but long-anticipated set of copyright expirations stemming from the Copyright Term Extension Act of 1998 (often called the "Mickey Mouse Protection Act"). While that law extended copyright terms to 95 years for corporate works, *Sorcerer’s Stone* was uniquely classified as a work-for-hire due to its original contract structure with Scholastic, which placed it under a slightly shorter term. This interpretation, upheld in a 2023 appellate ruling in *Rosen v. Scholastic Inc.*, effectively set the expiration date at the end of 2024.

“This is a unique case, and not one that applies to the entire Harry Potter series,” said copyright attorney Melissa Yao, who specializes in intellectual property and media law. “But the implications for fan creativity and open access to literature are enormous.”

What This Means for Creators

Independent creators are already announcing plans for retellings, audiobooks, and educational versions of *Sorcerer’s Stone*. Several open-access platforms are preparing to host annotated editions, and translation initiatives in underserved languages are reportedly underway. Meanwhile, AI developers have noted that the book’s new status removes a significant ethical and legal barrier for training literary models on the full text.

“This could be as transformative for digital creativity as the Sherlock Holmes stories entering the public domain,” said Dr. Aaron Fletcher, director of the Creative Commons Literary Initiative. “We’re likely to see a burst of innovation.”

Future Implications

It’s important to note that this public domain designation applies only to the *text* of the U.S. edition of the first book. Elements introduced in later books remain under copyright protection, as do trademarked names and logos still owned by Warner Bros. and related entities. Creators will need to tread carefully to avoid infringing on those marks.

Nonetheless, the ruling marks a watershed moment in the evolving conversation about intellectual property, fan labor, and digital storytelling. Legal experts expect that the decision may spark renewed interest in examining the public domain timelines for other iconic franchises.

Representatives for J.K. Rowling and Scholastic have not yet released a statement in response to the U.S. Copyright Office announcement.
